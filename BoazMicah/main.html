What really is the crystal structure of VI3, and why should we care?
Structural Properties of VI3

Ever since the successful isolation of a single sheet of graphene from bulk graphite in 2004, research into two-dimensional materials, where the layers are held by weak forces, has attracted a lot of research interest. This is mainly driven by the compelling properties of the individual atomic layers. With these materials, novel devices such as tunnelling transistors, resonant tunnelling diodes and light emitting diodes have been discovered. 2D materials have properties that are strikingly different from ther 3D parent compound, and their properties ranges from metals, semi-metals, semiconductors to insulators. Beacuse these layered materials are held by weak foces, the individual atomic layers can easily be separated and transferred onto a wide variety of subtrates, enabling easy integration with silicon technology that is technologically mature. However, 2D magnets have remained elusive, and was recently discovered in 2017. This was and still remains very exciting as it they offer the possibility of tuning their electronic and mechanical properties using an electric field or stain. This permits the conception of devices where the magnetism is controlled at will. The combination of 2D magnets and other 2D materials will probably result in structures with completely new and unexpected properties we can explore both theoretically and experimentally. The challenge now is to find 2D magnets at room temperature.

This article is about a project that investigated the structural properties of the layered semiconducting material, vanadium iodide. VI3 is confirmed to become magnetic around 50 K down to its monolayers in the literature. Also, it is accepted within the literature that the crystal structure of the compound changes around 80 K. This structural transition was also confirmed in this project. However, there are controversies surrounding the crystal struture of the material above and below the transition. Different studies report different structures in the high and low temperature regime. The VI3 material is of special importance because it offers more opportunities to study magnetism in layered structures and offers more possiblities for future 2D devices. The inconclusiveness of determinng the crystal structure of VI3 has limited our full understanfing of its properties and its potential as a monolayer magnetic material. To solve this, the project used all the reported and many unreported crystal structures to describe the compound. The aim was to find the best crystal structure that describes VI3 at all temperatures.
The high-temperature structure of VI3 is trigonal, with the best fitting space group being R-3. The compound transitions into the lower symmetry monoclinic structure below 80 K. The best fitting space group in the low temperature region is the C2/c space group.

Shiny crystals of vanadium iodide

The process of determining the crystal structure of the powdered VI3 starts by shining X-rays onto the material. The radiation scatters and carries information about the material. The scattered radiation is observed as a diffraction and it is unique to the material being irradiated. VI3 is a crystal, meaning it is made up of unit blocks that spread out through the whole crystal. Hence, describing this unit block is all that is needed to describe the whole crystal. The unit cell is described by its lenght in the X,Y and Z directions, and also the position of its atoms. A space group, which is a set of symmetry operations, is used to obtain the corrdinates of all the atoms in the crystal using the unit cell information. There are 230 possible space groups. The structural information about the unit cell and the space group are obtained from the diffraction pattern. This is done by creating a model that tries to match the diffraction pattern by refining the structural parameters. The specific space group when combined with the refined structural paramters that closely match the diffraction pattern is the crystal structure of the material. This process is called Rietveld refinement, and more details can be found here. The project used the Rietveld method to see how closely each of the reported crystal structures match the measured diffraction pattern. It was found that the R-3 rhombohedral space group best describes the crystal structure at high temperatures. The C2/c monoclinic space group best fits the X-ray diffraction pattern at low temperatures. This project helped clarify the confusion surrounding the crystal structure of vanadium iodide. With the crystal structure settled, future research projects can focus on the magnetic properties of the crystal. This may help pave the way to enable 2D magnets to be used as the architecture of the next-generation electronic devices.

Controversies Of the crystal structure of VI3
Suhan Son et al - 2019
Reported the room temperature trigonal structure P31c and a monoclinic crystal structure at low temperatures
Tai Kong et al - 2019
Determined the trigonal structure R-3(148) at 100 K and suggested a subtle structural phase transition.
Shangjie Tian et al - 2019
Claim a high temperature monoclinic structure C2/c and a low temperature rhombohedral structure R-3.
